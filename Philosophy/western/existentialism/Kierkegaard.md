## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. Life
Born in Copenhagen on May the 5th, 1813, during <u>the Danish Golden Age</u> a rich cultural period in Denmark. However Copenhagen was a poor city, the Danish state has gone bankrupt, only a few people who could preserve their property in these difficult times, include Kierkegaard's father.

From 1821 to 1830, Kierkegaard attended the School of Civic Virtue, where Kierkegaard studied and learned Latin, Greek, and history, among other subjects.
He went on to study theology at the University of Copenhagen.

The Concept of **Irony**
1. Socrates is *negative* in the sense that he refuses to present a positive thesis or doctrine, with concrete positive content. <u>Hegel's interpretation of Socrates.</u>
2. <u>The concept of Irony</u> : to undermine bourgeois society.
3. <u>Irony as a Controlled Element, the Truth of Irony</u>, his own view of the proper and appropriate use of irony.

### 1.2. Socratic
<p></p>

* Socratic **irony and ignorance**: Socrates pretending to be ignorant to encourage others, like Euthyphro, to explain their supposed expertise, often leading to Aporia (æ‚–è®º) and their embarrassment. which also make the reader or listener self-active.
  * Kierkegaard admired Socrates' approach, seeing it as a way to <u>challenge complacency and provoke critical thinking in society</u>.
* Socrates argued that he was not a **Sophist** by emphasizing his commitment to the pursuit of truth, the distinction between knowledge and opinion, the importance of moral integrity, and his unique method of inquiry.
* Socrates considered himself a **gadfly** because he aimed to provoke thought, challenge societal norms, and inspire self-examination among the citizens of Athens.
* Socrates' **daimon** serves as a negative force, preventing Socrates from making poor decisions, but does not provide positive guidance.
* Socrates practiced **maieutics**, likening his questioning technique to midwifery, helping others to discover truths within themselves (emphasizing that he <u>is not teaching anything</u>).
  * It is the individual's responsibility to seek answers. (since "we are born with ideas")

## 2. Kierkegaard, Martensen and Hegelianism

**Autonomy** is often equated with freedom, allowing individuals to make their own choices.
Autonomy also seen negative, for undermining tradition, community, and religious values.  

**Martensen** "On the Autonomy of Human Self-Consciousness" (1837) critiques the philosophies of Kant, Schleiermacher, and Hegel, focusing on the concept of autonomy.
* Martensen argues that these thinkers emphasize individual autonomy too heavily, neglecting the essential dependency of humans on God.
* He introduces the idea of <u>"modern autonomy,"</u> suggesting that while individual self-consciousness is important, it should not overshadow the recognition of divine influence and communal values, arguing for a balance with dependence on God and tradition.

**Hegel**'s investment on Socrates in *The History of Philosophy*, (and Kierkegaard's investment on Socratic Irony) using three main sources *The Philosopher Plato*, *The historian Xenophon*, *The comic writer Aristophanes*.
* Hegel portrays Socrates as a **pivotal moment** in the shift from the objective focus (understanding the natural world) of Pre-Socratic philosophers to <u>an inward examination of thought and self-awareness</u>.
* Traditional view of **nature law** - somethings are right or wrong by nature.
  * Hegel argues that Socrates introduced the idea that individuals must <u>attain truth through their own rational examination</u> (**subject truth**), challenging the acceptance of traditional customs and beliefs (**object truth**).
  * Kierkegaard 1846 develops this distinction : **Objective truth** of Christianity (determined by historical record, sources, and so far), **Subjective truth** - the individual's relation to Christianity.
* Hegel highlights Socrates' role in questioning **established norms**, suggesting that this critical approach laid the groundwork for modern conceptions of subjectivity and individual autonomy.
